The Mark of
the Compasses

1576, Christopher Plantin, an ambitious printer, relocated his printing business to a house on the Vrijdagmarkt in Antwerp. He renamed the house “The Golden Compasses,” a symbol of precision, labor, and stability.

The pair of compasses wasn't just in the name of his house. It was the symbol of his vision and the motto driving his revolutionary printing business.

The symbol

In 1557, Christopher Plantin adopted a powerful symbol that would come to define his business. The pair of compasses became his printmark. It would appear in various forms on the title pages of all Plantin Press books.

Click Drag the stamp and hover over the frame to uncover the iconic printmark!

The printmark came with a motto: 'Labore et Constantia' which perfectly captured the principles that drove him. This motto and the pair of compasses, fit perfectly together.

The compasses

Plantin selected the pair of compasses as his printer's mark, a tool with deep symbolic significance

Hover Press the words to reveal Plantins principles

The stationary point of the compasses represents constancy, the foundation essential to any effort. The moving arm, represents labor, the work required to achieve the results. Together, they inscribe a perfect circle, embodying harmony and balance.

The pair of compasses not only served as his business logo but as a daily promise of the principles he had.

Plantin did not merely wait passively for what fortune saw fit to drop in his lap. It was not without reason that in 1557 Plantin took as his motto Labore et Constantia, represented visually by the compasses. The stationary point stands for constancy, the moving point for labor.

The Motto

"With hard work
and patience you can overcome all setbacks"

The motto captures Plantin's belief that true greatness can only be achieved through tireless effort and steadfast dedication. As he explained in the introductory pages of his monumental Biblia Polyglotta, this motto was both a personal creed and a business promise.
